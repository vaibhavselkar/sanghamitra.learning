Understand the Author's Purpose

Read the short passage below and identify its Author's Purpose to improve your reading comprehension.

Passage:

In the late 19th century, the Industrial Revolution was transforming the economic and social fabric of societies across Europe and North America. Factories began to spring up in urban areas, drawing workers from rural communities in search of better opportunities. While industrialization brought significant advancements in production and technology, it also led to widespread challenges, such as harsh working conditions, child labor, and overcrowded living spaces.

Amid these changes, reformers and writers sought to address the moral and social implications of industrialization. Some, like Charles Dickens in his novels "Oliver Twist" and "Hard Times," vividly depicted the struggles of the working class, exposing the inequities of the industrial system. Others advocated for legislative reforms to improve workers’ rights and safety. For instance, labor unions began to form, pressuring governments to pass laws regulating working hours and conditions in factories.

While critics of industrialization argued that it exploited the most vulnerable members of society, proponents celebrated its ability to lift entire nations out of poverty. They highlighted the ways in which industrialization fostered innovation, increased access to goods, and laid the groundwork for modern infrastructure. The tension between these contrasting views remains a focal point of historical discussions on the Industrial Revolution, offering valuable insights into the costs and benefits of progress.

Strategies to Identify the Author's Purpose

Understanding the author’s purpose is key to interpreting a passage. Is the author trying to inform, persuade, entertain, or explain? Use the following strategies to sharpen your skills in identifying the author’s intent:

What is the Author's Purpose?

The author’s purpose is the primary reason or intent behind writing the passage. It reflects what the author aims to achieve—whether to inform, convince, entertain, or clarify a subject.

Strategies to Identify the Author's Purpose

Strategy 1: Identify the Tone and Language

The author’s choice of words, tone, and style can reveal their purpose:

  • Persuasive Tone: Uses emotional appeals, strong arguments, or calls to action.
  • Informative Tone: Focuses on providing facts, data, or explanations without bias.
  • Entertaining Tone: Incorporates humor, vivid imagery, or storytelling.
Strategy 2: Look for Keywords or Phrases

Keywords can often signal the author’s purpose:

  • Inform: "To explain," "demonstrates," "provides information."
  • Persuade: "Urge," "advocate for," "encourage action."
  • Entertain: "Describe," "engage," "tell a story."
Strategy 3: Consider the Context

Think about the broader context in which the passage was written:

  • Is the passage part of a news article? Likely written to inform.
  • Is it an advertisement? Likely written to persuade.
  • Is it a story or anecdote? Likely written to entertain.
Strategy 4: Identify the Central Goal

Ask yourself: "What does the author want me to do or think after reading this passage?"

  • Is the author aiming to change your opinion? Likely persuasive.
  • Does the author want to teach you something? Likely informative.
  • Is the author aiming to entertain or amuse you? Likely entertaining.
Strategy 5: Practice with a Detailed Example
Example Passage:

"Plastic pollution has become one of the most pressing environmental issues of our time. Over 8 million tons of plastic waste enter the oceans annually, harming marine life and ecosystems. Governments and organizations worldwide are working to tackle this crisis by implementing recycling programs and banning single-use plastics. However, individual actions are equally important. Simple steps like using reusable bags, bottles, and containers can significantly reduce your plastic footprint. By making these small changes, you can contribute to a cleaner, healthier planet."

Step-by-Step Strategy to Identify the Author's Purpose:
  1. Analyze the Tone:

    Tone: Concerned yet optimistic. The author emphasizes the severity of plastic pollution but provides practical solutions.

  2. Look for Keywords:

    Keywords like "reduce," "recycling programs," and "simple steps" suggest an intention to persuade readers to take action.

  3. Consider the Context:

    Context: The passage discusses an environmental issue, likely intended to raise awareness and encourage change.

  4. Identify the Goal:

    Goal: The author wants readers to take steps to reduce plastic usage and help the environment.

The Author's Purpose:

To persuade readers to take action against plastic pollution by adopting sustainable habits.

Why Is This the Author's Purpose?
  • The tone and keywords encourage action, not just information sharing.
  • The passage offers specific solutions, aiming to inspire behavior change.
  • The context (environmental issue) aligns with a persuasive intent.
