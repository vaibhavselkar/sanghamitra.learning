Understand the Author's Purpose

Read the short passage below and identify its Author's Purpose to improve your reading comprehension.

Passage:

In the early 20th century, urbanization and industrialization were transforming cities into bustling centers of commerce and innovation. Amidst this rapid change, public parks began to emerge as vital spaces for recreation and social interaction. City planners of the time believed that parks could improve the quality of urban life by providing a green refuge from the chaos of the industrialized world. They envisioned these areas not just as places of leisure, but as tools for promoting public health, fostering community ties, and encouraging moral behavior.

One notable example of this vision was Central Park in New York City. Designed by Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux in the 19th century, it became a template for other urban parks around the world. Olmsted believed that exposure to nature could soothe the human mind and counteract the stress of urban life. He deliberately designed meandering pathways and open spaces to invite strolling, contemplation, and social interaction. By providing a space where people from all walks of life could gather, Central Park also embodied ideals of equality and shared public benefit.

However, critics of the time argued that these parks often served the interests of the elite more than the general public. Wealthier citizens had easier access to the parks, while working-class neighborhoods were frequently neglected in urban planning. Some historians suggest that the grand vision of parks as tools for social reform was not always realized, and instead, they sometimes mirrored the inequalities of the societies they were meant to uplift.

Question:

Based on the passage, which of the following can be inferred about the role of urban parks in the early 20th century?

Strategies to Make Inferences

Making inferences involves reading between the lines and drawing conclusions based on what is implied, rather than what is directly stated. These strategies will help you develop this critical reading skill:

What is an Inference?

An inference is a logical conclusion based on evidence and reasoning. It involves using clues from the text combined with your own knowledge to understand what the author implies but does not state outright.

Strategies to Make Inferences

Strategy 1: Use Context Clues

Look for clues within the text to uncover implied information:

  • Definitions: Unfamiliar words or concepts might be explained indirectly.
  • Descriptions: Detailed descriptions of settings, characters, or events can provide hints.
  • Dialogue: What characters say and how they say it can reveal hidden emotions or motives.
Strategy 2: Connect Background Knowledge

Combine your prior knowledge with details from the text to draw conclusions:

  • Personal Experiences: Relate the situation in the text to your own experiences.
  • World Knowledge: Use your understanding of common concepts or situations.
  • Genre Expectations: Consider typical themes or settings in the genre to interpret meaning.
Strategy 3: Pay Attention to Character Actions and Reactions

Characters’ actions often reveal their thoughts and feelings:

  • Body Language: Physical reactions can indicate emotions not explicitly mentioned.
  • Decisions: Choices made by characters can reflect their priorities or conflicts.
  • Interactions: How characters interact with others can reveal underlying relationships.
Strategy 4: Analyze Cause and Effect

Look for cause-and-effect relationships to infer what might happen next or what has already happened:

  • Immediate Effects: Identify what occurs directly after an event.
  • Long-Term Effects: Consider potential future outcomes based on the events in the text.
  • Hidden Consequences: Think about consequences that may not be directly mentioned.
Strategy 5: Look for Implied Meaning

Not all information is stated directly. Use subtle clues to uncover implied meanings:

  • Unstated Emotions: Infer how a character might be feeling based on their actions or words.
  • Hidden Relationships: Identify relationships or connections that are not immediately clear.
  • Underlying Themes: Look for broader messages or themes that the author might be suggesting.
Strategy 6: Practice with a Detailed Example
Example Passage:

Emily glanced at the clock for the third time in ten minutes. The room was unusually quiet, save for the faint hum of the air conditioner. She tapped her pen nervously against the desk, her eyes darting to the empty chair across from her. Finally, she sighed and muttered, “I guess he’s not coming.”

Step-by-Step Strategy to Make Inferences:
  1. Use Context Clues:

    Emily’s repeated glances at the clock and nervous behavior suggest she is waiting for someone.

  2. Connect Background Knowledge:

    People often feel anxious or disappointed when someone they are waiting for doesn’t arrive.

  3. Pay Attention to Character Actions and Reactions:

    Emily’s sigh and the empty chair indicate she is realizing the person will not show up.

  4. Analyze Cause and Effect:

    The absence of the person she was expecting is causing her disappointment.

  5. Look for Implied Meaning:

    The passage implies that Emily had an important meeting or appointment, and the absence of the other person affects her.

What Can We Infer?

Emily was waiting for someone who didn’t show up, and this has left her feeling disappointed or frustrated.

Why Is This the Inference?
  • Her actions (glancing at the clock, tapping the pen) indicate nervousness.
  • The empty chair suggests she expected someone to be there.
  • Her sigh and words imply she has given up waiting.
